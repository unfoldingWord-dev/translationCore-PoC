Metaphor

This page answers the question: What is a metaphor and how can I translate a sentence that has one?

In order to understand this topic, it would be good to read:

  • Figures of Speech
  • Simile

Description

A metaphor occurs when someone speaks of something as if it were something else because there is something that they have in common. That is, in some way one is like the other. The thing he speaks of is called the topic. The thing he calls it is the image. The way that they are similar is the point of comparison.

1. Sometimes the topic and the image are both stated clearly.

Jesus said to them. “I am the bread of life. He who comes to me will not hunger, and he who believes on me will never thirst.“ (John 6:35 ULB)

Jesus called himself the bread of life. The topic is “I” and the image is “bread.” Bread is a food that people ate all the time. The point of comparison is that both are necessary to have life. Just as people need to eat food in order to have physical life, people need to trust in Jesus in order to have spiritual life.

2. Sometimes only the image is stated clearly.

Produce fruits that are worthy of repentance (Luke 3:8)

The image here is “fruits.” The topic is not stated, but the reader must understand that it is actions or behavior. Just as trees can produce good fruit or bad fruit, people can produce good behavior or bad behavior. The point of comparison is that both behavior and fruit reveal the nature of their source. Fruits that are worthy of repentance are good behavior that is appropriate for people who have repented.

3. The point of comparison is usually not stated clearly. It is up to the reader to think about the topic and the image and to ponder how the two things are similar. It is important to remember that in any one metaphor there is only one point of comparison. The topic and the image are usually very different things that are not similar in most ways. But the person making the metaphor wants the hearer or reader to think about the one way that they are similar.

Purposes of Metaphor

  • One purpose of metaphor is to teach people about something that they do not know (the topic) by showing that it is like something that they already know (the image).
  • Another purpose is to emphasize that something has a particular quality or to show that it has that quality in an extreme way.
  • Another purpose is to lead people to feel the same way about one thing as they would feel toward another.
  • Often metaphors are the favorite way for speakers of a language to talk about something. For example, when English speakers talk about the amount of something changing, they often say that it is going up or down. (“The literacy rate is going up.” “The price of bread has gone down.”) Many other languages require their speakers to say these things in other ways.

Reasons this is a translation issue

  • People may not realize that a word is being used as an image in a metaphor.
  • People may not be familiar with the thing that is used as an image.
  • If the topic is not stated, people may not know what the topic is.
  • People may not know what the point of comparison is, that is, how the topic and the image are alike.

Translation Principles

  • Make the meaning of a metaphor as clear to the target audience as it was to the original audience.
  • Do not make the meaning of a metaphor more clear to the target audience than it was to the original audience.
  • One of the benefits of using metaphor for teaching something is that it makes people think more deeply than they would otherwise. Try to retain this benefit in your translation.

Examples from the Bible

I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep. (John 10:11 ULB)

In the metaphor above, the topic is “I” and the image is “the good shepherd.” Jesus called himself the good shepherd. Jesus then helps the reader understand the metaphor by saying in what way the shepherd is good. A good shepherd saves the lives of his sheep even if it would put his own life in danger. In the same way, Jesus wanted to save his people and would die in order to do it.

Jesus said to them, “Take heed and beware of the yeast of the Pharisees and Sadducees.” The disciples reasoned among themselves and said, “It is because we took no bread.” (Matthew 16:6-7 ULB)

Jesus used a metaphor, but his disciples did not realize it. When he said “yeast,” they thought he was talking about bread, but “yeast” was the image in his metaphor, and the topic that he did not state was the teaching of the Pharisees and Sadducees. Since the disciples did not understand what Jesus meant, it would not be good to state clearly here what Jesus meant. The meaning of the metaphor is made clear in verse 12.

Translation Strategies

If people would understand the metaphor in the same way that the original readers would have understood it, go ahead and use it. If not, here are some other strategies.

  1. If the target audience would not realize that the phrase has a metaphor, then change the metaphor to a simile. Some languages do this by adding words such as “like” or “as.”
  2. If the target audience would not know the image, see Translate Unknowns for ideas on how to translate that image.
  3. If the target audience would not know the image, or if the image is not used in the same way, use an image from your own culture instead. Be sure that it is an image that could have been possible in Bible times.
  4. Or, if the target audience would not know the image, simply state the truth plainly that the metaphor was used to communicate.
  5. If the target audience would not know what the topic is, then state the topic clearly. (However, do not do this if the original audience did not know what the topic was.)
  6. If the target audience would not know the point of comparison, then state clearly how the topic is like the image.
  7. If the metaphor seems to be a normal way to say something in the the biblical language, then use the normal way that your language says that same thing.

Examples of Translation Strategies Applied

1. If the target audience would not realize that the phrase has a metaphor, then change the metaphor to a simile. Some languages do this by adding “like” or “as.”

  • I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep. (John 10:11)
    • “I am like a good shepherd. A good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep.” (John 10:11)

2. If the target audience would not know the image, see Translate Unknowns for ideas on how to translate that image.

  • Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me? It is hard for you to kick a goad. (Acts 26:14 ULB)
    • “Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me? It is hard for you to kick against a pointed stick.”

3. Or, if the target audience would not know the image, or if the image is not used in the same way, use an image from your own culture instead. Be sure that it is an image that could have been possible in Bible times.

  • his hand was leprous, as white as snow. (Exodus 4:6) - If people would not know what snow is, you could choose something else that is very white.
    • “his hand was leprous, as white as milk”
    • “his hand was leprous, as white as the moon”

4. Or, if the target audience would not know the image, simply state the truth that the metaphor was used to communicate.

  • I will make you become fishers of men. Mark 1:17
    • “I will make you become people who gather men”
    • “Now you gather fish. I will make you gather people.”

5. If the target audience would not know what the topic is, then state the topic clearly. (However, do not do this if the original audience did not know what the topic was.)

  • Produce fruits that are worthy of repentance (Luke 3:8 ULB)
    • “Produce actions that are worthy of repentance”

6. If the target audience would not know the point of comparison, then state clearly how the topic is like the image.

  • I am the light of the world. (John 9:5 ULB)
    • “I am the one who shows the world what is true just as light shows what is real.”
    • “I am the one who gives the light of truth to the world.”
  • Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me? It is hard for you to kick a goad. (Acts 26:14 ULB)
    • “Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me? You fight against me and hurt yourself like an ox that kicks against its owner's pointed stick.”

7. If the metaphor seems to be a normal way to say something in the the biblical language, then use the normal way that your language says that same thing.

  • For after David had in his own generation served the desires of God, he fell asleep, was laid with his fathers, and experienced decay, (Acts 13:36 ULB)
    • “For after David had in his own generation served the desires of God, he died, was laid with his fathers, and experienced decay,”
